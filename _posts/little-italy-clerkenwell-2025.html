Introduction

Saffron Hill:
Dickens's Darkest Street

Clerkenwell and the streets around Saffron Hill make up the core of what was once London's Little Italy — a community of Italian immigrants that arrived in waves from the early 19th century onwards, settled in one of the poorest and most overlooked corners of the city, and shaped British culture in ways that are still visible today.

Saffron Hill itself is described by Dickens in Oliver Twist as one of the most miserable locations in London — dark, muddy and essentially unpoliced. It sat at the boundary of several jurisdictions: the City of London on one side, the borough of St Pancras on another, and what had been a liberty — church land where the ancient right of sanctuary theoretically still applied — in between. Thieves and the vulnerable alike found it useful to be beyond anyone's clear authority.

The area was among the cheapest in London. The River Fleet ran nearby, underground but still influencing the dampness and smell of the streets above it. When the early 19th century Italian immigrants arrived — mostly artisans from the north of Italy, from around Como and the Liri valley — they came without much money and needed the cheapest housing available.

They were seen, even in this context, as a step up from the local population. Most had trades. Lens makers and instrument makers came from around Como, making telescopes, microscopes and eyewear — skilled, relatively well-paid work. Later waves from the south were poorer, often ice cream sellers and street vendors. The community was never homogeneous, and the tensions between northern and southern Italian immigrants ran alongside the larger tensions with the Irish population also living here.

Theme · Immigration

Two Waves:
Artisans & Then Labourers

The first wave of Italian immigrants to Clerkenwell in the early 19th century were predominantly skilled workers from northern Italy — instrument makers, lens grinders, craftsmen of various kinds. They were seen as respectable, had jobs, and integrated cautiously into the neighbourhood without displacing the existing community.

The second wave, from the mid-19th century onwards, came largely from the south — from Campania, Calabria, the Abruzzi. They were less skilled, less well paid, more likely to be selling from barrows and carts than working in workshops. The ice cream sellers came from this wave. So did the organ grinders — once so numerous that the expression "organ grinder" became common currency — and the street photographers and pavement artists.

The community that developed from these two waves filled the streets between Saffron Hill, Leather Lane and the area around the Italian church — a dense, Italophone neighbourhood in the middle of London, with its own newspaper, its own clubs, its own market on Leather Lane where Italian provisions could be reliably found. By the early 20th century there were significant numbers of gangsters among them as well.

Stop 1 · Gravel Lane / Arlington Way

Giuseppe Mazzini:
Nationalist, Exile, Founder of Schools

Mazzini Plaque · Arlington Way, EC1

Giuseppe Mazzini was born in Genoa in 1805, during the Napoleonic occupation. Italy in his lifetime was not a single country — it was a patchwork of kingdoms, duchies and papal territories, many of them under Austrian rule. Mazzini wanted a unified, republican, democratic and secular Italian state. His ideas influenced not just Garibaldi, who is better known internationally, but also Woodrow Wilson, Mahatma Gandhi and Jawaharlal Nehru. The concepts of national self-determination that shaped the 20th century owe something to Mazzini's 19th-century thinking.

Mazzini was a troublemaker — he said so himself, cheerfully. He led a series of failed insurrections in Italy aimed at unifying the country and throwing out the Austrians. Each failure landed him in exile: first Switzerland (who became nervous about his activities and arrested him), then France (same result), then London.

He lived here, on and off, at least four times. This building on Arlington Way is where he stayed. He fundraised for Italian nationalism, founded a secular Italian-language school for the children of immigrants living in the area, and hosted Garibaldi when the great general came to London to fundraise. The Garibaldi-Mazzini Club — originally a networking space for Italian nationalists, latterly a fundraising organisation — survived until 2008. It paid for the Arandora Star memorial in 2012. A plaque marks the house where Mazzini lived.

Stop 2 · Arlington Way

Negretti & Zambra:
Queen Victoria's Telescope Makers

The building now occupied by a jewellery shop was once the home of Negretti and Zambra, one of the most distinguished scientific instrument makers in Victorian London. They arrived in the 1850s, making specialist lenses and optical equipment — the kind of craftsmanship that northern Italian immigrants from the Como region were renowned for. Their instruments were used by Queen Victoria and Prince Albert, and then by Edward VII. The firm ran continuously until 1985, when it was finally taken over. Almost 130 years of precision instrument making from the same address in Clerkenwell.

Stop 3 · Farringdon Road

Carlo Gatti &
Ice Cream for Everyone

Carlo Gatti is possibly the most consequential figure in this story, and the least well known outside food history circles. He was Swiss-Italian, from the Ticino, and arrived in London in his thirties having tried various things in Paris first. London's economy was booming. He set up a hot chocolate and waffle shop — a high-end operation, comparable to the best specialist cafes you might find today. Then he got interested in ice cream.

Ice cream had been available in London since the 17th century, but only for the wealthy. Keeping it required vast quantities of ice, stored in deep basements, expensive to source and even more expensive to maintain. Gatti found a much cheaper method of producing ice cream in large quantities, and he imported ice from Norway in summer, storing it in the warehouses by the Regent's Canal near King's Cross. The Canal Museum is on this site today.

He exhibited his ice cream machine at the Great Exhibition of 1851. Queen Victoria tried it and bought one herself. Within a generation, ice cream had gone from a luxury available only to the rich to something anyone could buy from a barrow on any street in London. Gatti did not do this alone, but he was the most significant individual in the transition.

He diversified into dance halls, music venues, restaurants. He retired at 54 to Ticino, married a woman considerably younger than himself, and left the London business to his children and grandchildren. He was a multi-millionaire. The premise here burned down some years after he established it, but the insurance payout was generous and allowed him to expand considerably across London.

Stop 4 · Saffron Hill

Darby Sabini:
The Gangster from Saffron Hill

The Caribbean Club · Saffron Hill, EC1

The building now occupied by the Caribbean Club was, in the early 20th century, the headquarters of Darby Sabini — the most notorious gangster in London in his time, and possibly in the whole country. He was born on Saffron Hill in the 1880s, the son (possibly) of a man called Ottavio Sabini, and grew up in this neighbourhood, attending school just around the corner until he was thirteen.

He wanted to be a boxer but lacked the self-discipline. He became a bouncer in bars where boxing matches were held. In 1920, at a venue on Saffron Hill, he knocked out a South London gangster who had insulted an Italian waitress. That was the beginning. His headquarters were here. His reputation grew from protecting women and, more broadly, Italians, in the neighbourhood.

His primary income came from protection rackets at racecourses across southeastern England. His gang carried razors — the source of the term "razor gang" — and shook down bookmakers. His great rival, Billy Kimber (also featured in Peaky Blinders), was eventually outmanoeuvred, and several of Kimber's men went to prison after a confrontation with Sabini's people. Sabini imported Sicilian gunmen when he needed permanent solutions to problems.

He was affable and modest in person — wore ordinary clothes, was liked by the local children who called him Bob, reportedly kept the neighbourhood relatively orderly as a side effect of his dominance. His empire began to unravel in the 1930s, when Mussolini tried to court the Italian diaspora and the connection became a liability. During the Second World War, despite not speaking Italian and never having been to Italy, he was interned on the Isle of Man as an enemy alien for two years. Other gangs moved in while he was gone. He never fully recovered his position. He died in 1950.

Stop 5 · Clerkenwell Road

The Italian Church &
the Arandora Star

Church of Our Lady of Mount Carmel · Clerkenwell Road, EC1 · 1863

The Italian church on Clerkenwell Road was built in 1863 by an Irish architect, John Miller Rice, modelled on the Basilica of San Crisogono in Rome — a late Roman basilica of the fourth century, its forms revived by Victorian architects with a taste for Italian precedent. At the time, it was the largest Catholic church in London. It has been renovated several times since, most recently in the 1990s, and still draws Italian families from as far as Essex and Sussex on significant occasions.

On the exterior of the church is a memorial to the Arandora Star, unveiled in 2012. In 1940, following Italy's entry into the war, the British government interned thousands of Italians living in Britain as potential enemy aliens. Many were to be transported to Canada on a ship called the Arandora Star. It was torpedoed off the northwest coast of Ireland by a German U-boat. Approximately 800 people died — Italians, Germans (including some German Jewish refugees), and others.

Many of the bodies washed up on the Scottish islands or the coasts of Northern Ireland. Italy — particularly towns like Lucca and Parma, from which many of the victims had originally come — built memorials decades ago. In London, it took until 2012 for a memorial to appear. The Garibaldi-Mazzini Club, in one of its final acts before closing in 2008, raised the funds for it.

Next door to the church, Toni's delicatessen — first opened in 1919, the first delicatessen to open in England — recently became a restaurant and cafe after years as a purely provisions shop. Around the corner, a small Italian social club still operates, though its programming has shifted towards music events and launches.

The Sagra della Madonna del Carmine takes place each year on the Sunday nearest 16 July — a street fair that has run almost every year since the 1870s, the first public Catholic procession in England since the Reformation. Homemade wine, Italian food, the procession of the Madonna through the streets of Clerkenwell. Worth planning a visit around if you're in London in July.
Finale · Warner Street

The Sagra:
Since the 1870s, Almost Every Year

The walk ends at the Sagra — the street festival on Warner Street that marks the feast of Our Lady of Mount Carmel. Stalls selling Italian food, local producers, a few people selling homemade wine. The procession itself, when it comes, involves the statue of the Madonna being carried through the streets by members of the congregation, accompanied by a brass band and people from the community who have made the journey back to Clerkenwell for this one day each year, even though most of them no longer live here.

It is, as the guide notes, the first public Catholic procession in England since the Reformation. When it began in the 1870s, public Catholicism in this form was still sufficiently unusual that it required a degree of nerve. It stopped only during the two World Wars and during COVID. Everything else that Little Italy was — the tenements, the ice cream barrows, the Mazzini school, the organ grinders, the razor gangs — has gone. The Sagra is still here.

The Italian church is open most days and worth going inside. The nearby Leather Lane Market runs Monday to Friday as a working street market — food stalls at lunchtime are excellent. Toni's cafe next to the church is good for coffee and lunch. The organ builders at the end of Warner Street — the last remaining specialist of its kind in the area — occupy a Victorian workshop and occasionally show visitors around.
