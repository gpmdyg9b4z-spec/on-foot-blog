Introduction

Legal Deposit:
Why the British Library Can't Say No

The Treasures Tour at the British Library begins not with the treasures themselves but with something more fundamental: the reason the library has them at all, and the legal mechanism that ensures it keeps getting more. The guide's opening is characteristically dry — he notes that if you read five books a day from the British Library's collection, it would take you 100,000 years to finish. By which time, of course, the collection would have grown considerably further.

Legal deposit is the law requiring publishers to send a copy of everything they publish in the United Kingdom to the British Library. Not just books. Not just newspapers. Everything printed in the UK. Magazines. Catalogues. An IKEA catalogue published in the UK must be deposited. An Argos catalogue. Any printed matter, however mundane, if it was published here.

The guide offers a way to grasp the scale. If you were to stack up all the newspapers and magazines the British Library is legally required to acquire in a single month, the height of that stack would be equal to six double-decker buses.

The Legal Deposit Act, which began with books, has been expanded several times through Parliament and now also covers digital publications — websites, online journals, digital-only content. Around 150,000 books are published in the UK each year, each of which triggers a mandatory copy for the British Library. The collection grows by something in the region of three million items annually across all formats.

Theme · Scale

The Other Legal Deposit Libraries
& Why the British Library Is Biggest

The British Library is not the only legal deposit library in the UK. There are six in total: the British Library, the Bodleian in Oxford, Cambridge University Library, the National Library of Scotland, the National Library of Wales, and Trinity College Dublin. All six receive copies under the legal deposit provisions.

The difference is that the other five can, in practice, be selective. If a book arrives at the Bodleian and there is no space, or the subject is too obscure to justify retention, there are decisions to be made. The British Library cannot turn anything down. Everything published in the UK, they keep. This is why the British Library is considerably larger than any of the others, and why it keeps needing more basement space.

Theme · History

From the British Museum
to King's Cross: 250 Years

The British Library is, as a standalone institution, only 50 years old. But its collections are considerably older, because it grew out of the British Museum Library — and to understand how it got here requires going back to 1753.

In 1753, the British Museum was formed to house the collections of three men: Hans Sloane (the physician and collector whose Chelsea garden we know from elsewhere on these walks), Robert Cotton, and Robert Harley, Earl of Oxford. The idea — revolutionary for its time — was that the public should be able to come and see these great manuscripts and objects, free of charge. The British Museum Library became, in effect, the first public library in Britain.

For 200 years, the books and manuscripts that now form the British Library's treasures collection were at the British Museum. The famous circular Reading Room — the domed space where Karl Marx, Lenin, Gandhi and Virginia Woolf all worked — is still there, at the British Museum. Visitors who arrive at the British Library asking to see the Round Reading Room are looking in the wrong place. That room stayed with the museum.

In 1973, the British Library Act was passed, creating a separate national library. The books from the British Museum were transferred here — or rather, they were eventually transferred here, because the building at King's Cross was not yet built. The British Library spent several years in temporary accommodation while the site was developed.

Theme · The Building

The Site: What Was Here Before
& What the Government Paid

British Library · 96 Euston Road, N1 · Opened 1998 · Colin St John Wilson

The government acquired this site in 1975. It was, at the time, a goods yard adjacent to St Pancras station — not quite wasteland, but a functional industrial site serving the railway. The government paid around £7 million for it.

The British Library was not originally going to be here. The first plan was to build it adjacent to the British Museum, in Bloomsbury. This would have been the obvious location — keep the library near the museum it was coming from, in the neighbourhood where it had always been. The plan was scrapped at the eleventh hour because building it would have required demolishing some listed buildings in Bloomsbury. Given what the British Museum looks like, the guide suggests, this was probably the right decision.

The building at King's Cross was designed by Colin St John Wilson and took an unusually long time — from commission to completion, the project spanned around 30 years. It opened in 1998, making it one of the longest-running building projects in modern British history. It is Grade I listed.

Theme · The Collection

Where the Books Actually Are:
Under Your Feet

The guide saves what he considers his best detail for the transition between the introductory talk and the Treasures Gallery itself. Visitors arriving at the British Library walk across the piazza at ground level, see the building, perhaps look up at the external brick and the tower, and walk in. The guide asks them to think about where the 12 million books are — because what is visible above ground is clearly not large enough to hold them.

"When you were standing outside on the piazza before you came in, you were actually standing on the collection. Half of it is under your feet — in our four basements."

— British Library Treasures Tour Guide

The British Library has four basement levels extending beneath the piazza. The collection is stored largely underground, in controlled conditions of temperature and humidity, retrieved by automated systems when readers request items. The visible building above ground — impressive as it is — houses the reading rooms, the galleries, the staff offices and the public spaces. The books themselves are mostly below the surface, beneath the feet of people walking in from Euston Road.

British Library Treasures (Sir John Ritblat Gallery): Free entry. Open daily. Contains the Lindisfarne Gospels, the Gutenberg Bible, the Magna Carta, Lewis Carroll's Alice's Adventures Underground (the handwritten original), Beatles lyrics handwritten on an envelope, and many other items. One of the finest free cultural experiences in London. Photography is permitted in most of the gallery without flash. Guided Treasures Tours run regularly — book in advance on the British Library website.
Coda

The Collection That
Keeps Growing

The Treasures Tour ends at the entrance to the Sir John Ritblat Gallery — named after the property developer who made a significant donation to the library — where the most famous items in the collection are displayed. The guide lists some of them: the Lindisfarne Gospels, one of the greatest works of early medieval art; two copies of Magna Carta; the Gutenberg Bible; original musical manuscripts; maps and globes; and the handwritten originals of texts that most people have only ever encountered in print.

These treasures came from those original three collections — Sloane, Cotton and Harley — assembled in the 18th century with the explicit aim of making them available to the public. The first public library in Britain, the guide notes, was not a civic institution built by local government but a national institution assembled from private collections and opened to anyone who wanted to come.

The collection is 250 years old, has 12 million books, and acquires roughly three million more items every year. It is, by any measure, one of the great accumulations of human knowledge — and half of it is in the basement, under the piazza, below street level, waiting to be read.
